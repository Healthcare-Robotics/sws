1Georgia Institute of Technology 2Emory University

Stretch with stretch is a system that enables a mobile manipulator to lead exercise games for physical therapy.

Abstract

Physical Therapy (PT) is a key component of many rehabilitation regimens. Administering PT exercises in a clinical setting can be a cognitively and physically demanding process for physical therapists. Additionally, user adherence to self-guided PT "homework" outside of the clinic is often inconsistent. To address these issues, we present Stretch with Stretch (SWS), an interactive robotic system for leading stretching exercise games.

SWS consists of a mobile manipulator that physically and verbally guides users through physical therapy exercises. The robot's soft end effector serves as a target for users to reach towards and press on with a hand, foot, or knee. The location of these targets is customized for individuals in three ways: first, based on individuals' body dimensions; second, based on direct sampling of individuals' range of motion; and third, by dynamically adjusting the target's distance from the user based on the user's performance. Additionally, the system includes sound effects and verbal feedback from the robot to keep users engaged throughout an exercise session. Through a user study ($n=5$) of individuals with Parkinson's Disease (PD), we demonstrate that SWS an effective and engaging platform for leading exercises related to the treatment of PD.

Mobile Manipulator that leads exercise

Sws is a robotic system for physical therapy with a dynamic exercise difficulty adjustment system. This system adapts to individuals in three ways: first, by estimating a user's range of motion using simple kinematic models; second, by directly sampling a user's range of motion through haptic interaction; and third, by adapting the difficulty of an exercise based on models of a user's performance.

Personalized to Human kinematic Model

Sws can personalized the exercise to the user's body dimension. The robot can estimate the user's body dimension by measuring the user's height and arm length. The robot can also directly sample the user's range of motion by asking the user to reach the robot's end effector. The robot can then adjust the exercise to the user's body dimension.

User Study and Analysis

Through a user study (`n=10`), we show that people with PD have a positive impression of SWS, and perceive it as useful and easy to use. By analyzing video ratings of the exercise sessions, assessed by a movement scientist, we demonstrate that individuals suffering from PD are typically capable of correctly executing exercises led by a robot. This demonstrates the feasibility of robot-led physical therapy.
